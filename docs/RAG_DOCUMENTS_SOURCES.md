## 1. Procedural Guides - How to File FIR

### **Official Sources Found:**

- Provides authoritative legal procedure information
- Toll-free helpline: **15100** (urgent legal assistance across India)

#### **Bureau of Police Research & Development (BPR&D)**
- Official SOP on Zero FIR & e-FIR (2023): https://bprd.nic.in/
- Government-verified procedures

#### **State Legal Services Authorities**
- Delhi State Legal Services Authority: https://dslsa.org/
- Delhi legal advice: **1516** (24√ó7 toll-free)
- Karnataka SLSA: https://kslsa.kar.nic.in/

### **Key FIR Filing Information (2024 Updates)**

#### **Zero FIR (Effective July 2024 - BNSS Implementation)**
Under Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) enacted July 2024:
- Any police station can file FIR regardless of jurisdiction
- Victim can walk into nearest police station
- Police MUST transfer to correct jurisdiction

#### **Online FIR (e-FIR)**
Available in major cities:
- Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru have online platforms
- For non-urgent cases (theft, lost items)
- Portal access via state police websites

#### **Step-by-Step FIR Procedure:**
1. Visit nearest police station
2. Approach duty officer
3. Provide incident details (written or verbal)
4. Review statement carefully before signing
5. Get free FIR copy with:
   - FIR number
   - Date of registration
   - Police station name
   - Investigating officer details
6. Follow up weekly for investigation progress

#### **If Police Refuse to Register FIR:**
- Approach Superintendent of Police (SP)
- File complaint with Judicial Magistrate under Section 156(3) CrPC
- Contact State Human Rights Commission
- Use RTI (Right to Information) to file complaint

#### **Critical Rights:**
- FIR copy must be given **FREE** immediately
- No fees for FIR registration or investigation
- Right to file in English or Hindi
- Right to read and correct FIR before signing
- Police MUST register FIR for cognizable offences

## 2. Evidence Collection - Theft Cases

### **Types of Evidence Required:**

#### **Documentary Evidence (Strongest)**
1. **Purchase Proof:**
   - Invoices/receipts
   - Serial numbers (IMEI for phones)
   - Warranty cards
   - Insurance documents

2. **Financial Records:**
   - Bank statements (if cash stolen)
   - UPI transaction history
   - Credit card statements

3. **Ownership Proof:**
   - Bills of sale
   - Registration documents
   - Photos of items before theft

#### **Digital Evidence**
1. **CCTV Footage (Critical!):**
   - **Time-sensitive:** Often deleted after 7-30 days
   - Request immediately from:
     - Your cameras
     - Neighbor cameras
     - Street/shop cameras
   - Save to multiple USB drives
   - Note: camera owner details, date, time, location

2. **Authentication Requirements (BSA 2023):**
   - Section 63(4) certificate required for CCTV footage
   - Must verify:
     - CCTV system was functioning properly
     - Footage not altered/tampered
     - Proper extraction and preservation methods
   - **Without certificate = inadmissible in court**

3. **Mobile/Digital Records:**
   - Call logs (if threats made)
   - SMS/WhatsApp screenshots
   - Location history (Google Maps timeline)
   - Photos/videos taken

#### **Physical Evidence**
- Don't touch crime scene
- Photograph everything before cleaning
- Let police collect:
  - Fingerprints
  - Footprints
  - Broken locks/windows
  - Tools left behind

#### **Witness Evidence**
- Name, contact, address of all witnesses
- Types:
  - Eyewitnesses (saw theft happen)
  - Discovery witnesses (found theft with you)
  - Character witnesses (confirm ownership)
  - Expert witnesses (item valuation)

### **Preservation Guidelines:**

- Make multiple copies of all documents
- Store digital evidence in 3 places (cloud + USB + email)
- Keep chronological organization
- Label everything with dates
- Take photos before handing to police
- Provide only copies to police (keep originals)

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Edit or enhance photos/videos (tampering)

- Touch crime scene before police

## 3. Victim Rights & Compensation

### **Constitutional Rights in Criminal Cases**

#### **Right to File FIR (CrPC/BNSS)**
- Police MUST register FIR for cognizable offences
- Cannot refuse based on jurisdiction (must transfer)
- Cannot refuse based on "small amount"
- Cannot ask for "lawyer opinion" first
- FIR copy must be FREE

#### **Right to Information**
- Know investigation progress
- Know when accused arrested
- Know when chargesheet filed
- Get copy of chargesheet

#### **Right to Legal Assistance**
- Engage lawyer at any stage
- Free legal aid (if eligible)
- Lawyer presence during statements

### **Victim Compensation Scheme**

#### **Legal Framework:**
- **Section 357A CrPC** (inserted 2008, effective Dec 31, 2009)
- **Section 396 BNSS** (since July 1, 2024)
- Based on Malimath Committee Report

#### **2024 Updates:**

- **Supreme Court 2023 order:** All payments within **60 days** of application
- **Interim relief:** ‚Çπ1 lakh for rape within 15 days

- Financial aid for various crimes

- Based on severity and victim age

**Delhi - Avlamban Fund Scheme (2024):**
- Permanent corpus: **‚Çπ10 crore**
- For acid attack survivors rehabilitation

#### **Compensation Process:**
1. **Apply to:** District Legal Services Authority (DLSA)
2. **Attach:**

   - Loss documentation
3. **Timeline:**
   - Hearing: 30-60 days

#### **Contact Information:**

### **Free Legal Aid Eligibility (2024)**

#### **How to Apply:**

## 4. Case Law - Criminal Breach of Trust

### **Legal Framework:**
- **IPC Section 406** (old) = **BNS Section 316(2)** (2024)
- **Punishment:** Up to 3 years imprisonment, or fine, or both

### **Essential Elements (Must Prove All):**

1. **Entrustment**
   - Property was entrusted to accused
   - Can be written or oral (written is stronger)
   - Fiduciary relationship existed

2. **Dishonest Intention**
   - Intent to cause wrongful loss
   - Intent present at time of entrustment or later
   - Not mere breach of contract

3. **Misappropriation or Conversion**
   - Unauthorized use of property
   - Refusal to return after demand
   - Attempts to conceal

### **Landmark Supreme Court Judgments:**

#### **1. Delhi Race Club (2024) - Recent SC Ruling**
**Held:** "Entrustment is quintessential for BNS-316"
**Key Point:** Mere non-fulfillment of contract ‚â† criminal breach of trust
**Lesson:** Prosecution must prove property was specifically entrusted

#### **2. State of Gujarat v. Jaswantlal Nathalal (1968 AIR 700)**
**Held:** Breaking contract or misusing property alone doesn't constitute criminal breach
**Key Point:** Must prove dishonest intent clearly
**Lesson:** Intent matters more than just wrong use

#### **3. Rashmi Kumar vs Mahesh Kumar Bhada**
**Context:** Stridhan property (woman's property)
**Held:** If woman's property transferred to husband/in-laws and they misappropriate = BNS-316
**Key Point:** Stridhan unequivocally belongs to woman
**Application:** Applies to property entrusted to family members

#### **4. Ramaswamy Nadar v. State of Madras**
**Held:** Mere possession/control ‚â† entrustment
**Key Point:** Must be clear fiduciary relationship
**Lesson:** Document the entrustment relationship clearly

#### **5. Lalita Choudhury v. State (2007)**
**Facts:** Woman gave ‚Çπ5 lakhs to relative for "safe keeping"; relative invested without permission
**Held:** This IS breach of trust (specific purpose: safe keeping violated)
**Lesson:** Specify purpose of entrustment explicitly

### **Evidence That Works:**

#### **Strong Evidence (Courts Accept):**
‚úÖ Written agreement/contract showing entrustment
‚úÖ Emails/WhatsApp discussing purpose
‚úÖ Bank statements proving transfer
‚úÖ Invoices showing personal use of entrusted funds
‚úÖ Accounting books showing shortage
‚úÖ Registered demand notices
‚úÖ Witness to entrustment transaction
‚úÖ Accused's recorded admission

#### **Weak Evidence (Often Rejected):**
‚ùå Oral claims without corroboration
‚ùå Unsigned documents
‚ùå Estimates without records
‚ùå Hearsay testimony
‚ùå Mixed business-personal dealings

### **Common Defense Arguments & Court Responses:**

| Defense | Court Response |
|---------|---------------|
| "It was a loan, not entrustment" | Examines nature of transaction, looks for specific purpose, checks trust relationship |
| "I used temporarily, planned to return" | Intent at time of taking matters; later return doesn't erase crime (may reduce sentence) |
| "No demand was made before FIR" | Demand strengthens case but not always essential; implied demand acceptable |

### **Conviction Data:**

**Overall Success Rate:** 40-45% (NCRB data)

**High Conviction (>70%) When:**
- Clear written entrustment agreement
- Documentary proof of misappropriation
- Accused admits during investigation
- Substantial amount with strong evidence

**Low Conviction (<30%) When:**
- Oral entrustment only
- Mixed personal-business relationship
- Accused claims it was a loan
- No demand notice before FIR

**Average Timeline:**
- Investigation: 3-6 months
- Trial: 18-36 months
- Appeals: 1-3 years (if appealed)
- **Total: 3-6 years average**

### **Punishment Patterns by Amount:**

| Amount | Typical Punishment | Reference |
|--------|-------------------|-----------|
| < ‚Çπ50,000 | Fine only or 6 months imprisonment | State v. Ramesh (2015) |
| ‚Çπ50,000 - ‚Çπ5 lakhs | 1-2 years + fine | Gupta v. State (2017) |
| ‚Çπ5 lakhs - ‚Çπ50 lakhs | 2-3 years + fine | Shah v. State (2019) |
| > ‚Çπ50 lakhs | 3 years + substantial fine | Mehta v. State (2020) |

**Leniency Factors:**
- First-time offender
- Property returned during trial
- Genuine remorse shown
- No past criminal record

**Example:** Sharma v. State (2018)
- Employee took ‚Çπ2 lakhs
- Returned ‚Çπ1.5 lakhs during trial
- Expressed remorse
- **Result:** 6 months (suspended) + ‚Çπ50,000 fine

### **Compounding (Legal Settlement):**
BNS-316 is **compoundable with court permission**

**Process:**
1. Parties agree on settlement terms
2. Submit joint application to court
3. Court verifies settlement is voluntary
4. Court grants permission
5. Case dismissed with restitution order

**Typical Settlement:**
- Full return of property/money
- Interest on delayed payment (8-12% p.a.)
- Victim withdraws complaint
- Mutual release

## 5. Common Mistakes When Filing FIR

### **Timing Mistakes:**

‚ùå **Delaying FIR Filing**
- CCTV deleted after 7-30 days
- Witnesses forget details
- Accused may flee
- Weakens credibility

‚úÖ **What to Do:**
- File within 24 hours
- If unavoidable delay, explain reason in FIR

### **Documentation Mistakes:**

‚ùå **Vague Descriptions**
Bad examples:
- "Some cash was stolen"
- "My phone was taken"
- "Jewelry was missing"

‚úÖ **Specific Descriptions**
Good examples:
- "‚Çπ15,000 in cash (three ‚Çπ500 notes, rest in ‚Çπ100 notes)"
- "Samsung Galaxy S21, IMEI 123456789012345, purchased Jan 15, 2023 from Amazon"
- "22-carat gold necklace, 50 grams, valued ‚Çπ2.5 lakhs, bill attached"

‚ùå **Exaggerating Value/Items**
Consequences:
- Entire FIR credibility lost
- Counter-case for false FIR (BNS-211)
- Insurance claim rejected
- Accused acquitted on contradictions

‚úÖ **Conservative Estimation:**
- List only actually stolen items
- Estimate value conservatively
- Provide purchase proof
- Better to underestimate

### **Procedural Mistakes:**

‚ùå **Not Reading FIR Before Signing**
- Officer may write differently
- No proof of what you said
- Hard to correct later

‚úÖ **Careful Review:**
- Read complete FIR
- Demand corrections if needed
- Get FIR copy immediately
- Note FIR number and IO name

‚ùå **Touching Crime Scene**
- Destroys fingerprints
- Disturbs physical evidence
- Can't reconstruct events

‚úÖ **Preserve Scene:**
- Don't touch anything
- Take photos/videos immediately
- Let police collect forensic evidence

### **Follow-up Mistakes:**

‚ùå **Not Following Up**
- Investigation may stall
- Evidence may not be collected
- Case may be closed

‚úÖ **Active Follow-up:**
- Visit police station weekly
- Call investigating officer
- Provide additional evidence if found
- Maintain cordial relationship

‚ùå **Discussing Case Publicly**
- Accused prepares defense
- Witnesses influenced/threatened
- Social media posts used against you

‚úÖ **Confidentiality:**
- Keep details private
- Only discuss with lawyer/police
- Don't post on social media
- Don't confront accused

### **Critical Misunderstandings:**

| Myth | Reality |
|------|---------|
| "Police won't file FIR for small amounts" | ALL thefts are cognizable; police MUST file FIR regardless of amount |
| "I need proof to file FIR" | FIR is for REPORTING, not proving; investigation comes after |
| "If accused returns property, case closed" | Crime already occurred; returning may reduce sentence, doesn't cancel crime |
| "Can't file FIR after insurance claim" | Can file FIR anytime; insurance doesn't prevent criminal case |

### **Red Flags - Get Lawyer Immediately:**

üö© Police refuse to file FIR
üö© Police suggest "compromise" before investigation
üö© Accused is powerful/influential
üö© Property value > ‚Çπ1 lakh
üö© Accused threatens you/family
üö© False counter-accusation
üö© Investigation officer asks for "expenses" (bribe)
üö© You know accused well (business partner, relative, employee)
